# Positioning messaging framework example

## Scenario

Company: DispatchGuard

Product: Incident coordination software for B2B logistics teams

Fictional context: Mid-market cold-chain logistics providers coordinate urgent delivery exceptions across email, spreadsheets, text messages, and phone calls. The cost of a missed handoff is not just internal chaos. It can mean spoiled inventory, SLA penalties, angry customers, and dispatch managers who stop trusting the system.

## 1. Executive summary

DispatchGuard should position itself as the incident coordination layer for cold-chain logistics teams that need to protect on-time delivery, product safety, and customer trust when exceptions happen. The sharpest frame is not "workflow automation" or "AI operations." The real decision is whether dispatch leaders keep coordinating high-risk exceptions manually or use a shared system that makes ownership, escalation, and customer impact visible before a shipment is lost.

## 2. Target segment and persona

Segment:
- Mid-market cold-chain logistics providers handling food, pharma, or temperature-sensitive goods
- 200 to 2,000 employees
- Regional or national operations with multiple dispatch locations
- High SLA pressure and frequent delivery exceptions

Persona:
- VP Operations
- Director of Dispatch
- Regional Operations Manager

Company attributes:
- Runs time-sensitive deliveries where delay creates financial or safety consequences
- Coordinates across dispatch, drivers, warehouse teams, and customer support
- Has outgrown email, spreadsheets, and tribal knowledge
- Not yet ready for a multi-year enterprise control tower implementation

Optimizing for:
- Uptime of the delivery operation
- Shipment safety and reliability
- Faster exception resolution
- Fewer SLA misses and customer escalations
- Confidence to take on more complex customer commitments

Not optimizing for:
- Generic task management
- Broad enterprise transformation
- Lowest-cost project tracking
- Replacing the transportation management system

Out of scope:
- Small local courier teams with low exception volume
- Parcel networks with mature internal control towers
- Teams looking only for driver route optimization

## 3. Real alternative

Status quo:
- Shared inboxes
- Spreadsheets
- Text threads
- Phone calls
- Manual check-ins during daily standups

Why customers choose it today:
- Everyone already knows how to use it
- It feels flexible during messy exceptions
- No procurement cycle is needed
- Managers can force progress through personal follow-up

Why it breaks:
- No single owner for an exception
- Escalations happen after the SLA is already at risk
- Temperature, customer, and delivery data live in separate systems
- New dispatchers cannot see how similar incidents were resolved
- Leaders find out too late to save the shipment or reset expectations

## 4. Pains, consequences, and JTBD

Pains:
- Dispatchers chase updates across five channels
- Managers do not know which exceptions are truly urgent
- Customer support gives vague or late updates
- Repeat incidents are handled from scratch every time
- Teams rely on the most experienced dispatcher to remember what to do

Business consequences:
- Missed SLAs and penalty fees
- Spoiled or rejected shipments
- Lost customer trust
- More overtime during exception-heavy weeks
- Slower onboarding for new dispatchers
- Leaders avoid saying yes to higher-value, more complex customer requirements

Job to be done:
- When a high-risk delivery exception appears, help operations teams coordinate the right response quickly, assign clear ownership, and protect the customer commitment before the shipment fails.

Emotional stakes:
- Dispatch leaders want to feel in control during messy days
- They want confidence that the team can handle exceptions without heroics
- They want to say yes to demanding customers without fearing operational chaos

## 5. Differentiated value

Core value proposition:
- DispatchGuard helps cold-chain logistics teams resolve high-risk delivery exceptions before they become SLA failures, rejected shipments, or customer escalations.

Differentiated capabilities or model:
- Exception-specific workflows for cold-chain logistics
- Shared incident timeline across dispatch, warehouse, driver, and customer support teams
- Risk scoring based on SLA window, temperature sensitivity, customer priority, and delay reason
- Clear owner, next step, and escalation path for every exception
- Playbooks based on similar past incidents

Business outcomes:
- Fewer missed SLAs
- Faster exception resolution
- Lower spoilage and rejection risk
- Less overtime spent chasing updates
- More reliable customer communication

Emotional outcome:
- Operations leaders feel confident that exceptions are visible, owned, and recoverable.

## 6. Positioning statement

Template version:

For dispatch and operations leaders at mid-market cold-chain logistics providers, who want fewer SLA misses and rejected shipments while feeling in control during high-pressure delivery exceptions, DispatchGuard is the incident coordination platform that lets teams identify, own, and resolve high-risk exceptions before they become customer failures so they can protect revenue and customer trust. Unlike shared inboxes, spreadsheets, and text threads that create late escalations because ownership and risk are scattered across channels, DispatchGuard helps teams coordinate exception response with cold-chain-specific workflows, risk scoring, and shared incident timelines so they can save more shipments and say yes to demanding customers with confidence.

Tightened version:

DispatchGuard helps mid-market cold-chain logistics teams save at-risk shipments before they become SLA failures. Instead of chasing exceptions across inboxes, spreadsheets, and text threads, dispatch leaders get one shared incident workflow with clear ownership, cold-chain risk scoring, and escalation paths so teams can protect customer commitments and operate with confidence on messy days.

## 8. Reasons to believe

Proof or proof paths:
- Demo a live incident view showing owner, SLA risk, temperature sensitivity, next step, and customer impact
- Compare one exception handled through email versus one handled through DispatchGuard
- Show sample incident timeline exported for customer support or postmortem review
- Customer story: a team saved a temperature-sensitive shipment because the right manager saw the risk before the SLA breach
- Metrics to validate: time to assign owner, time to escalate, SLA misses, rejected shipments, customer escalation rate, overtime hours during exception spikes

Mechanism:
- DispatchGuard is not a generic task board. It is built around the operating model of cold-chain exception response: urgency, ownership, escalation, customer impact, and repeatable playbooks.

## 10. Pressure-test notes

What is sharp:
- The segment is specific: mid-market cold-chain logistics providers
- The alternative is concrete: inboxes, spreadsheets, texts, phone calls
- The value ladders to uptime, safety, reliability, SLA protection, and customer trust
- The product is framed as a tool for saving shipments, not as generic workflow software
- The message has a sales-ready story: teams do not fail from lack of effort; they fail because risk, ownership, and context are scattered

What is still weak or unproven:
- Quantified impact is missing
- Need proof that risk scoring predicts the right incidents
- Need evidence that customers see this as urgent enough to buy now
- Need competitive comparison against TMS modules or enterprise control tower tools

Customer evidence that would improve confidence:
- Interview quotes about missed handoffs and late escalations
- Data on SLA penalties or rejected shipment costs
- Before/after metrics from pilots
- Examples of customer requirements the company could not accept before DispatchGuard
- Sales-call language showing buyers describe the pain as shipment risk, not just coordination friction
