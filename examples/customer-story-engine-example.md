# Customer Story Engine Example

## Prompt

Use the `customer-story-engine` skill to turn this compact raw customer note into a story brief, story spine, website customer story draft, one-page PDF outline, and sales proof. Do not invent proof beyond the raw details.

## Product context

Product: aviation operations software used by airport teams to coordinate fueling requests, line-service work, and operational handoffs.

Audience: airport operations leaders responsible for fuel sales, dispatch, customer service, and reliable handoffs during departure windows.

## Raw customer note

```text
Customer type: Regional airport operations director

Before: Fuel requests came in by phone, radio, and email. The front desk wrote some requests in a shared spreadsheet and kept sticky notes near the desk. If it was urgent, dispatch texted the line crew. The process mostly worked on normal days, but it depended on memory and people checking multiple places.

Trigger: A corporate flight almost left without topping off. The pilot called 20 minutes before departure asking if fuel was done. It was not. The team scrambled and saved the flight, but the director said it made the airport look disorganized.

Change after product: Requests are now in one place. The line crew can see the queue. The front desk can confirm status without chasing someone on the radio.

Result: They avoided another near-miss like that flight. The director said the team has more confidence during busy departure windows. No specific dollar amount was provided.

Customer language:
- "We were living off sticky notes and memory."
- "The scary part wasn't the missed fuel. It was realizing we had no clean handoff."
- "I don't need another dashboard. I need to know the plane isn't waiting on us."
- "If we miss a fuel request, that's real money and a relationship hit."

Objections before buying:
- They worried the crew would not adopt a new system.
- They did not know if the problem was big enough to justify software.
- They were concerned it would slow down the front desk.

Permission status: Unknown. Treat as internal draft unless approved.
```

## 3. Website customer story draft

### Headline

From sticky notes to a clear fuel queue: how one airport team fixed a risky handoff

### Short summary

A regional airport team was managing fuel requests through phone, radio, email, a spreadsheet, sticky notes, and memory. That worked until a corporate flight almost left without topping off. After moving requests into one place, the team could see the queue, confirm status faster, and work with more confidence during busy departure windows.

### The old way

Fuel requests did not arrive in one clean path.

Some came by phone. Some came over the radio. Others came through email. The front desk wrote some requests into a shared spreadsheet and kept sticky notes near the desk. When something was urgent, dispatch texted the line crew.

On a normal day, the process could hold together. But it depended on people remembering, checking multiple places, and handing off the right information at the right time.

As the director put it: "We were living off sticky notes and memory."

### The moment it became urgent

The problem became real when a corporate flight almost left without topping off.

The pilot called 20 minutes before departure to ask if fuel was done. It was not. The team scrambled and saved the flight, but the incident exposed the weak point in the process.

"The scary part wasn't the missed fuel," the director said. "It was realizing we had no clean handoff."

A missed fuel request was not just an operations issue. It could mean lost revenue and a damaged customer relationship.

### What changed

The team moved fuel requests into one place.

The line crew could see the queue. The front desk could check status without chasing someone on the radio. The work no longer depended on sticky notes, memory, and urgent texts to hold the handoff together.

This was not about adding another dashboard. It was about making sure the right people could see the work that needed to happen before the aircraft was waiting.

### The result

The team avoided another near-miss like that corporate flight. The director said the team has more confidence during busy departure windows.

No dollar amount was provided, so this story should not claim a specific amount saved or revenue increase. The supported result is simpler and still useful: fewer worries about missed handoffs when fuel requests matter most.

### Plain call to action

If fuel requests are still spread across phone calls, radio, email, spreadsheets, and sticky notes, start by looking at the handoff. The question is not whether the old way works on a quiet day. The question is what happens when a plane is waiting.

### Objection-handling proof

Objection: "Our current process works well enough."

Response:

That is usually true on normal days. In this story, the old process worked until it did not. The issue was not that people were careless. The issue was that fuel requests lived across phone, radio, email, sticky notes, a spreadsheet, texts, and memory. One near-missed corporate flight made the handoff risk visible.

Objection: "We do not need another dashboard."

Response:

The customer said the same thing: "I don't need another dashboard. I need to know the plane isn't waiting on us." The value is not a dashboard. It is a clearer handoff for work that affects departures, fuel sales, and relationships.

Objection: "The crew may not adopt a new system."

Response:

Keep the proof focused on the workflow: requests in one place, line crew can see the queue, and the front desk can confirm status without chasing radio updates. Do not overclaim adoption without more evidence.
