# Sales narrative deck example

## Example request

Use the `sales-narrative-deck` skill to create a first-call deck outline for a B2B SaaS product.

Product context:

- Product: WorkflowAI, an AI workflow governance platform for operations teams.
- Audience: VP Operations, RevOps leaders, and operations practitioners at 500-5,000 employee B2B companies.
- Problem: Teams are adopting AI tools in pockets, but leaders cannot see what workflows are automated, which outputs are trusted, or where risk is entering business processes.
- Current solutions: Spreadsheets, Slack threads, ad hoc approvals, IT ticketing, vendor-specific admin consoles.
- Differentiation: Gives operations teams one governed workspace to design, approve, monitor, and improve AI-assisted workflows.
- Sales motion: First discovery call with light product demo.
- Proof available: Two beta customers, early time-saved metrics, one quote from a RevOps director.

## First-call deck outline

### Deck purpose and audience

Purpose: Help operations leaders see AI workflow sprawl as an operations problem they need to own, then show how WorkflowAI gives them a practical way to govern and improve AI-assisted work.

Primary audience: VP Operations and RevOps leaders.

Secondary audience: Operations practitioners who build, manage, or troubleshoot workflows.

### Core narrative

AI is already changing how work gets done, but most operations teams are managing the change with spreadsheets, Slack, and disconnected admin tools. That creates a gap between what leaders need to trust and what practitioners can actually control. WorkflowAI gives operations teams a single place to design, approve, monitor, and improve AI-assisted workflows, so leaders get visibility and practitioners get a faster, safer way to work.

### Assumptions and missing proof

- Assumption: Buyer already has multiple teams experimenting with AI tools.
- Assumption: Operations, not only IT, is accountable for workflow quality and business process outcomes.
- Proof gap: Need quantified risk, error, or rework impact from current AI workflow sprawl.
- Proof gap: Need stronger customer story from a named customer when available.

## Slides

### 1. AI workflows are already running inside your business

Goal: Open with relevance and make the buyer feel understood.

Suggested content:

- Teams are using AI to draft, summarize, route, and decide work.
- Most of it is happening outside a shared operating model.
- Operations leaders are being asked to create control without slowing teams down.

Talk track notes: "You probably do not have one big AI rollout. You have dozens of small workflow changes showing up across teams. The question is whether operations can see and improve them before they become business-critical."

Visual direction: Simple icon map of departments using AI in different workflows.

Discovery question: "Where are you seeing AI show up in day-to-day workflows today?"

Proof placeholder: Customer quote about AI adoption spreading faster than governance.

### 2. The current operating model was not built for AI-assisted work

Goal: Name the pain and current solutions before pitching the product.

Suggested content:

- Spreadsheets track workflow owners but not workflow behavior.
- Slack approvals disappear after decisions are made.
- Vendor admin consoles show tool usage, not business process health.

Talk track notes: "The issue is not that teams lack tools. It is that every tool gives you a partial view. Operations ends up stitching together the truth after something breaks."

Visual direction: Before-state diagram showing spreadsheet + Slack + tickets + admin consoles.

Discovery question: "How do you currently know whether an AI-assisted workflow is approved, working, and still safe to use?"

Proof placeholder: Screenshot or anonymized example of a manual tracking process.

### 3. The hidden cost is not tool sprawl. It is workflow uncertainty.

Goal: Teach the buyer how to think about the problem.

Suggested content:

- Leaders cannot tell which AI workflows are trusted.
- Practitioners do not know which version, prompt, or approval path to use.
- Teams spend time auditing work instead of improving it.

Talk track notes: "Most companies describe this as AI tool sprawl. We think the sharper problem is workflow uncertainty: nobody is fully sure what is running, who approved it, or whether the output can be trusted."

Visual direction: Problem/category slide with the phrase "workflow uncertainty" centered.

Discovery question: "When something goes wrong in a workflow, how hard is it to trace what happened?"

Proof placeholder: Metric showing audit or rework time.

### 4. Operations needs a governed workspace for AI workflows

Goal: Define the new standard and set up the solution.

Suggested content:

- One place to define how AI should support a workflow.
- One approval trail for who changed what and why.
- One view of performance, exceptions, and improvement opportunities.

Talk track notes: "The goal is not to block AI use. The goal is to give operations the same muscle it already has for process quality, now adapted to AI-assisted work."

Visual direction: Three-part framework: design, approve, monitor.

Discovery question: "Who would need to be involved in approving a workflow like this at your company?"

### 5. WorkflowAI helps operations teams design, approve, and monitor AI-assisted work

Goal: Say what the product is and connect it directly to the problem.

Suggested content:

- Design workflow steps, prompts, owners, and success criteria.
- Route approvals across operations, IT, legal, or business owners.
- Monitor workflow usage, exceptions, and output quality over time.

Talk track notes: "WorkflowAI is a governed workspace for the operational side of AI adoption. It helps the people responsible for business processes make AI workflows visible, approved, and improvable."

Visual direction: Product shot or simplified workflow canvas.

Proof placeholder: Screenshot from current product.

### 6. Demo: from scattered AI process to governed workflow

Goal: Get to product early and make the story concrete.

Demo moment:

- Show a realistic RevOps workflow: inbound lead enrichment and routing.
- Use realistic data: lead source, company size, enrichment confidence, routing recommendation, approval status, owner, exception notes.
- Show three actions:
  1. Create or review the AI-assisted workflow.
  2. Approve the workflow with required stakeholders.
  3. Monitor exceptions and improve the workflow.

Talk track notes: "I will keep this to one workflow so we can focus on how the operating model changes, not every feature in the product."

Discovery questions during demo:

- "Who owns this workflow today?"
- "Where would your team want a human approval step?"
- "What would make this output trusted enough to use automatically?"

Pause and confirm: "Does this match how your team thinks about ownership and approvals, or would your process look different?"

Transition back to deck: "So the product is not just documenting the workflow. It is creating the control layer operations needs as AI becomes part of the workflow itself."

Proof placeholder: Beta customer workflow screenshot or anonymized demo data.

### 7. What teams get back: visibility for leaders, control for practitioners

Goal: Translate product into value for both buyer levels.

Suggested content:

- Leaders see which AI workflows are live, approved, and performing.
- Practitioners get a clear place to manage changes and exceptions.
- Teams reduce rework, audit time, and ad hoc approval loops.

Talk track notes: "For leaders, this is about confidence. For practitioners, it is about having a usable system instead of another compliance spreadsheet. You need both for the motion to stick."

Visual direction: Two-column leader/practitioner value slide.

Proof placeholder: Early beta metric, such as hours saved in workflow review.

### 8. Early customers are using WorkflowAI to turn AI experiments into managed operations

Goal: Add proof without overclaiming.

Suggested content:

- Beta customer A: consolidated workflow approvals from Slack and spreadsheets.
- Beta customer B: improved visibility into AI-assisted RevOps processes.
- Early quote: "We finally have one place to see which AI workflows are approved and where they need attention." — RevOps Director

Talk track notes: "These are early customers, so we will be specific about what we have seen and careful about what we have not proven yet. The pattern is clear: the first value is moving from scattered experiments to managed workflows."

Visual direction: Customer story cards with quote and metric placeholders.

Proof placeholder: Replace with approved customer names, logos, metrics, and quote source.

### 9. Why WorkflowAI instead of spreadsheets, tickets, or vendor consoles

Goal: Address competition and status quo.

Suggested content:

- Spreadsheets document workflows but do not operate them.
- Ticketing systems route requests but do not monitor workflow quality.
- Vendor consoles manage tools but not cross-functional business processes.

Talk track notes: "Most alternatives solve a piece of the problem. WorkflowAI is built around the business workflow, which is the level operations actually has to manage."

Visual direction: Competitive/status quo comparison table.

Discovery question: "Which of these systems would your team be most likely to use if you did not buy a dedicated platform?"

### 10. A practical path to start: one workflow, one team, measurable control

Goal: Make adoption feel achievable and set up next steps.

Suggested content:

- Start with one high-volume AI-assisted workflow.
- Define approval owners, exception rules, and success metrics.
- Expand once the team proves visibility, control, and time saved.

Talk track notes: "We do not recommend boiling the ocean. The fastest path is one workflow where the pain is visible and the operating model can be proven."

Visual direction: Pilot path timeline: select workflow, configure, approve, monitor, review.

Proof placeholder: Implementation timeline, pilot criteria, pricing/package shape.

### 11. Recap: make AI workflows visible, approved, and improvable

Goal: End with a strong memory and clear CTA.

Suggested content:

- AI workflow adoption is outpacing the current operating model.
- Operations needs one governed workspace for design, approval, and monitoring.
- WorkflowAI helps leaders trust the work and practitioners improve it.

Talk track notes: "If this is relevant, the next conversation should not be a generic product tour. It should be a working session around one workflow your team needs to govern."

Visual direction: Recap slide with three icons: visible, approved, improvable.

CTA: Schedule a 45-minute workflow mapping session with the RevOps owner and one practitioner. Bring one AI-assisted workflow that is high-volume, high-risk, or currently managed in spreadsheets.

## Optional appendix slides

### Pricing shape

- Pilot package for one workflow or one team.
- Platform package for multiple workflows and stakeholder groups.
- Enterprise package for governance, integrations, security, and reporting needs.

### Security and governance

- Data handling overview.
- Role-based access controls.
- Audit trail and approval history.

### Implementation

- Week 1: workflow selection and success criteria.
- Week 2: configuration and stakeholder review.
- Week 3: monitor, tune, and report results.

### Deeper proof

- Beta customer story.
- Before/after workflow map.
- Time-saved or rework-reduced metric.

## Final CTA

"If this problem is worth exploring, let's pick one workflow and map how it works today. From there, we can show exactly where WorkflowAI would add visibility, approvals, and monitoring — and whether there is a business case to move forward."
