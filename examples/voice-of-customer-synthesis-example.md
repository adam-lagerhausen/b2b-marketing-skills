# Voice of Customer Synthesis Example

## Prompt

Use the `voice-of-customer-synthesis` skill to synthesize these raw customer notes into PMM-ready inputs: pains, triggers, current workaround/status quo, JTBD, exact language, objections, proof, messaging implications, and unknowns.

## Product context

Product: aviation operations software used by airport teams to coordinate fueling requests, line-service work, and operational handoffs.

Target audience: airport operations leaders and teams responsible for fuel sales, dispatch, and customer service.

## Raw customer-note snippet

```text
Customer: Regional airport operations director

Before this, fuel requests came in by phone, radio, and email. If the front desk was busy or someone forgot to write it down, the line crew might not see the request until the plane was already waiting.

The trigger was a corporate flight that almost left without topping off. The pilot called 20 minutes before departure asking if fuel was done. It wasn't. We scrambled and saved the flight, but it made us look disorganized.

They were not actively shopping for software before that. The team had talked about improving the process, but the old way mostly worked until it didn't.

The director cares about not losing fuel sales and not disappointing based aircraft customers. The line crew cares about knowing what needs to happen without checking three places.

Current workaround was a shared spreadsheet plus sticky notes at the desk. Dispatch also texted people when something was urgent.

What changed with the product: requests are in one place, the line crew can see the queue, and the front desk can confirm status without chasing someone on the radio.

Customer language:
- "We were living off sticky notes and memory."
- "The scary part wasn't the missed fuel. It was realizing we had no clean handoff."
- "I don't need another dashboard. I need to know the plane isn't waiting on us."
- "If we miss a fuel request, that's real money and a relationship hit."

Objections before buying:
- They worried the crew would not adopt a new system.
- They did not know if the problem was big enough to justify software.
- They were concerned it would slow down the front desk.

Outcome mentioned:
- They avoided another near-miss like that flight.
- The director said the team has more confidence during busy departure windows.
- No specific dollar amount was provided.
```

## Synthesized output

### 1. Executive summary

- The core pain is not "task management." It is unreliable operational handoff during time-sensitive fueling work.
- The real competition was the status quo: phone, radio, email, shared spreadsheet, sticky notes, memory, and urgent texts.
- The buyer moment was reactive. A near-missed fuel request exposed risk that had been tolerated because the old process "mostly worked until it didn't."
- The decision-maker cares about fuel sales, customer relationships, and the airport looking organized. The user cares about seeing the work queue without checking three places.
- The strongest message should focus on confidence that aircraft are not waiting on the team, not on generic dashboard or productivity language.

### 2. Audience and buying roles

- User: Line crew and front desk staff coordinating fuel requests and status updates.
- Decision-maker: Regional airport operations director.
- Economic buyer: Not explicitly stated. Likely the operations director or airport leadership, but this needs validation.
- Champion: Operations director, because the near-miss created urgency and personal accountability.
- Blockers or influencers: Line crew and front desk, because adoption concerns could stall the purchase.
- Confidence level: Medium. User and decision-maker are clear; economic buyer is inferred.

### 3. Pains

| Pain | Evidence from notes | Business impact | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fuel requests get lost across channels | "phone, radio, and email" plus spreadsheet, sticky notes, and texts | Missed work, rushed service, poor customer experience | High |
| Handoffs are unreliable | "no clean handoff" and line crew may not see the request | Operational risk during departures | High |
| Staff must check too many places | Line crew wants to know what needs to happen "without checking three places" | Slower execution and more coordination burden | High |
| Management lacks confidence during busy windows | Director wants to know "the plane isn't waiting on us" | Anxiety, reputation risk, leadership escalation risk | Medium |
| Missed fuel requests threaten revenue and relationships | "real money and a relationship hit" | Lost fuel sale and customer trust | High |

### 4. Triggers

- Situation: Fuel requests came through disconnected channels and were tracked through spreadsheets, sticky notes, memory, and urgent texts.
- Trigger: A corporate flight almost left without topping off; the pilot called 20 minutes before departure and the team had to scramble.
- Reactive or proactive: Reactive.
- Why now: The incident made an accepted workaround feel risky and visible. It showed the team could lose revenue and damage an important customer relationship.
- Evidence: "The old way mostly worked until it didn't" and "It made us look disorganized."

### 5. Current workaround / status quo

- What they do today: Phone, radio, email, shared spreadsheet, sticky notes at the desk, dispatch texts, and team memory.
- Why it persists: It is familiar, low-cost, and good enough on normal days.
- Where it breaks: Busy departure windows, front-desk overload, urgent requests, and handoffs between front desk, dispatch, and line crew.
- Real competition: The make-do process, not another named software vendor.

### 6. Jobs To Be Done

- Functional job: Capture fuel requests in one place and make the current queue visible to the people doing the work.
- Emotional job: Feel confident that no aircraft is waiting because of an internal miss.
- Social job: Look organized and reliable to pilots, based aircraft customers, and airport leadership.
- JTBD statement: When fuel requests are coming in across phone, radio, and email during busy departure windows, I need one reliable place to capture, assign, and confirm the work, so I can protect fuel revenue and know the plane is not waiting on us.

### 7. Exact customer language

| Quote or phrase | Why it matters | Potential use |
|---|---|---|
| "We were living off sticky notes and memory." | Plain-language description of the status quo | Problem narrative, sales opener, campaign hook |
| "The scary part wasn't the missed fuel. It was realizing we had no clean handoff." | Reframes the pain from one incident to a systemic process gap | Sales discovery and executive messaging |
| "I don't need another dashboard. I need to know the plane isn't waiting on us." | Rejects generic product framing and gives the emotional outcome | Homepage/message direction; avoid dashboard-led copy |
| "If we miss a fuel request, that's real money and a relationship hit." | Connects operational miss to business impact | Value messaging and ROI discovery |
| "Mostly worked until it didn't." | Explains why status quo persists until a trigger event | Campaign angle for reactive buyers |

### 8. Objections and risks

| Objection | Who holds it | Why it matters | Proof needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| The crew may not adopt a new system | Operations director, line crew | If adoption fails, the product becomes another place to check | Simple workflow demo, onboarding plan, crew-facing proof |
| The problem may not be big enough for software | Operations director / economic buyer | Status quo feels free until a miss happens | Before/after workflow, cost of missed fuel request, risk narrative |
| It could slow down the front desk | Front desk, operations director | The product must reduce coordination burden, not add admin work | Demo showing fast request capture and status confirmation |
| Generic dashboard fatigue | Decision-maker | "Dashboard" language may weaken relevance | Position around reliable handoff and work visibility, not reporting |

### 9. Proof and outcomes

- Stated result: The team avoided another similar near-miss.
- Stated result: The director said the team has more confidence during busy departure windows.
- Quantified proof: None provided. Do not claim a specific dollar amount saved, time saved, fuel revenue increase, or adoption rate from this note alone.
- Anecdotal proof: The near-missed corporate flight is a usable story seed if the customer agrees and details are validated.
- Proof gaps: Number of fuel requests, frequency of missed or delayed requests before purchase, fuel revenue at risk, time saved, adoption level, and customer satisfaction impact.

### 10. Messaging implications

- Recommended message: Stop relying on sticky notes, memory, and radio follow-ups for fuel requests. Give your team one reliable handoff so aircraft are not waiting and fuel sales do not slip through the cracks.
- What to avoid: Do not lead with "dashboard," "task management," "workflow optimization," or broad operational efficiency claims. The customer is buying confidence in the handoff.
- Sales discovery questions:
  - How do fuel requests come in today?
  - Where does the handoff usually break?
  - What happens when the front desk is busy or the line crew misses an update?
  - Have you had a flight, fuel sale, or customer relationship put at risk by a missed request?
  - Who needs to trust the new process before you would change it?
- Content or campaign angles:
  - "Mostly worked until it didn't": the hidden risk in manual fuel-request handoffs.
  - From sticky notes to one fuel queue: how airport teams prevent missed requests.
  - The real cost of a missed fuel handoff is not only the fuel sale.
- Follow-up research needed:
  - Interview line crew users to confirm adoption barriers.
  - Quantify how often requests are missed, delayed, or chased manually.
  - Validate whether operations directors or airport executives own budget.
  - Gather a before/after metric before turning this into a case study.

### 11. Unknowns and validation plan

| Unknown | Why it matters | How to validate |
|---|---|---|
| Who signs the purchase | Determines economic-buyer messaging | Ask sales/customer who approved budget |
| Frequency of missed requests before purchase | Determines urgency and ROI case | Review historical incidents or interview operations team |
| Dollar value of fuel sales at risk | Needed before making revenue claims | Estimate average fuel sale and missed/delayed request rate with customer input |
| Adoption by line crew | Needed to overcome buyer objection | Interview crew, review usage data, or collect rollout notes |
| Whether this pattern repeats across airports | Determines broader positioning confidence | Synthesize more customer calls and win/loss notes |
