# Demo Storyline

## When to use

Use this skill when you need to plan a live or recorded B2B SaaS demo that advances a sales conversation.

Best fit for:

- First-call product demos
- Discovery-led demos after an initial qualification conversation
- Founder-led demos that need to become repeatable
- AE or SE demo talk tracks
- Custom interactive demos for a target segment or account
- Demo sections inside sales narrative decks
- Demo prep for vertical, persona, or use-case-specific sales motions

Do not use this skill to create a feature tour, product training script, help documentation, or generic click path. A strong demo should map to buyer pain, not inventory the product.

## Inputs

Ask for or infer as much of the following as possible:

- Target audience: buyer, practitioner, economic buyer, technical evaluator, industry, company size
- Buyer pain: what is broken, slow, expensive, risky, confusing, or newly urgent
- Current workflow: spreadsheets, legacy tools, manual process, services, internal workaround, competitor
- Desired outcome: what the buyer wants to improve, avoid, prove, or make easier
- Product area: the specific workflow, capability, or use case to demo
- Sales stage: first call, deep discovery, technical validation, executive demo, renewal, expansion
- Qualification goal: what the rep needs to learn or confirm during the demo
- Demo environment: available screens, data, integrations, personas, records, and constraints
- Realistic data: company names, user roles, customer records, pipeline values, dates, tasks, metrics, statuses
- Proof: customer story, metric, quote, benchmark, or before/after result
- Objections or likely confusion: implementation, integrations, data quality, security, adoption, ROI, differentiation
- Next step: stakeholder meeting, pilot, mutual action plan, technical validation, business case, proposal

If inputs are thin, produce a useful draft with explicit assumptions and gaps. Never fill the demo with generic placeholders like "Item 1," "Customer A," or "Task 2" when realistic examples can be invented or requested.

## Demo storyline workflow

1. Anchor the demo in buyer pain.
   - Start with what the buyer is trying to fix, not what the product can do.
   - Name the current workflow and why it fails.
   - Define the outcome the demo should prove.

2. Tell them what you are going to show.
   - Preview the scenario in plain English.
   - Explain the roles, data, and workflow they will see.
   - Set expectations: "I will show three moments, then we will pause and decide if this maps to your process."

3. Get to product early.
   - Use only enough setup to make the demo relevant.
   - Move into the product before the conversation becomes a lecture.
   - Keep slides or context brief unless the buyer needs framing.

4. Show one coherent workflow.
   - Build the demo around a realistic buyer scenario.
   - Show the before/after change in how work gets done.
   - Use specific data: names, accounts, dollar values, dates, segments, statuses, exceptions, owners.
   - Avoid showing every feature, setting, admin page, and edge case.

5. Pause after key steps.
   - Demos are hard to follow; slow down after important moments.
   - Confirm the audience follows before moving on.
   - If people look confused, stop and clarify instead of clicking faster.
   - Zoom in where needed: data, workflow step, decision logic, output, or business impact.

6. Ask discovery questions throughout.
   - Use each product moment to learn something about the buyer's process.
   - Treat conversation as a win, not an interruption.
   - If the buyer opens up, follow the thread before returning to the script.

7. Tell them what they saw.
   - Recap the workflow in terms of pain, value, proof, and next step.
   - Use a recap slide or summary section at the end.
   - Connect the product moment back to the original business problem.

## Demo script structure

Return the demo as a practical script a rep or founder could use live.

Include:

- Demo objective
- Target audience and assumed pain
- Qualification goal
- Scenario setup
- Realistic demo data
- Opening talk track: tell them what they are going to see
- Step-by-step demo flow, with each step containing:
  - Product moment
  - Buyer pain it maps to
  - What to say
  - What to click or show
  - Discovery question
  - Pause/checkpoint question
  - Likely confusion or objection
  - How to address it
- Recap slide or recap talk track
- Qualification signals
- Recommended next step
- Assumptions and missing proof

## Discovery questions

Use discovery questions at every major step. Good demo questions uncover fit, urgency, process, value, and buying path.

Examples:

- "How does your team handle this today?"
- "Where does this usually break down?"
- "Who owns this workflow when something goes wrong?"
- "What happens if this step is delayed or missed?"
- "Which system is the source of truth today?"
- "What data would your team need to trust this recommendation?"
- "Who needs to approve this before it becomes the standard process?"
- "What would make this valuable enough to prioritize this quarter?"
- "Where would you want human review versus automation?"
- "Is this close to how your team works, or would your process look different?"

## Qualification signals

Listen for signals that the demo is creating a real sales conversation.

Strong positive signals:

- Buyer describes their current process in detail
- Buyer corrects the demo scenario to match their real workflow
- Buyer names a cost, risk, delay, or missed revenue impact
- Buyer asks how this would work with their data, systems, roles, or approval process
- Buyer brings up other stakeholders who need to see it
- Buyer asks about implementation, pilot scope, pricing, security, or timeline
- Buyer compares the product against a real status quo or competitor
- Buyer says the demo reflects a current initiative or executive priority

Weak or negative signals:

- Buyer only asks generic feature questions
- Buyer cannot name who owns the problem
- Buyer says the current workaround is good enough
- Buyer engages with the category but not the workflow
- Buyer wants a recording but no next conversation
- Buyer is curious but has no pain, urgency, authority, or project

Use qualification signals to shape the recap and next step. A custom interactive demo can also reduce unqualified tire-kicker leads by making the buyer engage with a concrete workflow before sales spends more time; Reevo used this kind of custom interactive demo motion to reduce unqualified tire-kicker leads by 50%, improving sales efficiency and pipeline quality.

## Recap output

End every demo with a recap slide or spoken recap.

Default recap structure:

1. Pain confirmed
   - "You are trying to reduce [specific pain] caused by [current workflow/status quo]."

2. What the demo showed
   - "We showed how [product] helps [persona] move from [old way] to [new way]."

3. Value
   - "The practical value is [time saved, risk reduced, revenue protected, quality improved, visibility created]."

4. Open questions
   - "We still need to validate [integration, data, stakeholder, security, workflow variation, ROI]."

5. Recommended next step
   - "The right next step is [pilot scoping, stakeholder demo, technical validation, business case, proposal]."

## Quality bar

The demo storyline is good when:

- The demo starts with buyer pain and current workflow, not product features.
- The product appears early enough to create belief.
- The scenario is specific enough to feel real.
- Every step maps to a buyer pain, outcome, or qualification goal.
- The demo uses realistic data instead of generic placeholders.
- The talk track follows: tell them what you will show, show them, tell them what they saw.
- The rep pauses after key steps and checks whether the audience follows.
- Discovery questions are embedded throughout the demo, not saved for the end.
- The script gives the rep permission to stop, clarify, and follow buyer conversation.
- The ending recap ties the demo back to pain, value, proof, and next step.
- The demo would help qualify the account, not just impress the audience.

## Anti-patterns

Avoid:

- Feature tours with no buyer pain
- Long company or category lectures before showing product
- Generic demo data like "Item 1," "Acme," "Test User," or "Task 2"
- Showing every feature because it exists
- Moving too fast because the rep knows the product well
- Treating buyer questions as interruptions
- Ignoring confused faces or vague silence
- Demoing edge cases before proving the main workflow
- Over-automating the story when the buyer needs trust and control
- Ending with "Any questions?" instead of a recap and concrete next step
- Claiming proof that is not available
