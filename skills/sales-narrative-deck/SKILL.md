# Sales Narrative Deck

## When to use

Use this skill when you need to turn positioning, customer research, sales feedback, or product context into a customer-facing sales narrative deck.

Best fit for:

- First-call or discovery-call decks
- Value decks for account executives
- Founder-led sales decks that need to become repeatable
- New product or new buyer GTM stories
- Category, problem, or point-of-view narratives
- Demo-adjacent sales decks where the product should appear early
- Competitive or pricing-ready sales materials

Do not use this skill to make a brand manifesto, investor pitch, internal strategy deck, or generic product tour. The output should help a rep win a real customer conversation.

## Inputs

Ask for or infer as much of the following as possible:

- Target audience: buyer, practitioner, influencer, economic buyer, industry, company size
- Audience pains: what is broken, risky, slow, expensive, frustrating, or newly urgent
- Audience needs: what they need to achieve, avoid, prove, or change
- Current solutions: status quo, spreadsheets, internal workarounds, legacy tools, services, competitors
- Product: what you sell, core capabilities, integrations, packaging, pricing motion
- Category or market point of view: the problem you want to own
- Differentiation: why this approach wins versus alternatives
- Proof: customer stories, metrics, logos, quotes, case studies, analyst proof, screenshots
- Sales motion: first call, follow-up, demo, enterprise cycle, transactional motion, land-and-expand
- Objections: budget, priority, change management, incumbent tools, security, IT, ROI
- Demo assets: product areas to show, realistic data, screenshots, flows, or demo environment
- Constraints: deck length, required slides, brand tone, vertical, competitor names, pricing inclusion

If the inputs are thin, produce a useful draft but mark assumptions and proof gaps clearly.

## Workflow

1. Identify the real audience.
   - Separate practitioners from leaders.
   - Serve practitioners with specificity, but help reps win deals with leaders.
   - If a new product targets a different buyer, do not reuse the old GTM story. Name the different problems, pains, needs, current solutions, and sales motion.

2. Start with customer truth.
   - Lead with the problem, not the product.
   - Describe the audience's pains, needs, and current solutions before saying what you sell.
   - Use the buyer's language where available.
   - Own the problem or category the company wants to be associated with.

3. Teach before pitching.
   - Explain the shift in the market, the hidden cost of the status quo, or the new standard buyers should expect.
   - Make the buyer feel smarter and seen before asking them to believe the solution.

4. Introduce the product as the logical answer.
   - Connect every product claim to a previously established pain or need.
   - Focus on what reps can sell now.
   - Put fast-follow assets, roadmap, and future vision in notes or appendix unless they are essential to the deal.

5. Build a simple sales story.
   - Follow a clear beginning, middle, and end.
   - Make the first impression strong: the opening slides should immediately signal relevance.
   - Make the ending strong: recap the value and ask for a concrete next step.
   - Ensure each slide relates to the one before it.
   - Avoid more than three bullets per slide.

6. Design for live selling.
   - Write slide titles as the story, not labels.
   - Include talk track notes that a rep can actually say.
   - Add proof placeholders where evidence is needed.
   - Include customer stories, quotes, icons, product shots, pricing, competitive context, and solution slides when they help the rep advance the conversation.

7. Plan the demo moment.
   - Get to product early enough that the buyer is not waiting through a long lecture.
   - Use realistic data and scenarios.
   - Pause and confirm the audience follows.
   - Ask discovery questions during the demo.
   - Address confusion directly.
   - End with a recap slide that ties the demo back to the pain, value, and next step.

## Output format

Return a practical deck outline, not polished copy pretending to be final design.

Include:

- Deck purpose and audience
- Core narrative in one paragraph
- Assumptions and missing proof
- Slide-by-slide outline with:
  - Slide number
  - Slide title written as a message
  - Slide goal
  - Suggested content, with no more than three bullets where possible
  - Talk track notes
  - Visual direction, such as icon, diagram, product shot, customer quote, proof, or pricing table
  - Discovery question where useful
  - Proof placeholder where needed
- Demo moment:
  - Where it happens
  - What to show
  - What realistic data to use
  - Questions to ask
  - How to transition back to the deck
- Recap and CTA
- Optional appendix slides for pricing, competition, security, implementation, roadmap, or deeper customer proof

## Slide and narrative structure

Default first-call deck sequence:

1. Relevance opener
   - Show that you understand the buyer's world.
   - Name the audience and problem clearly.

2. Problem today
   - Explain the practical pain and business impact.
   - Include current solutions and why they fall short.

3. What changed
   - Teach the market shift or new expectation.
   - Make the problem feel urgent without fearmongering.

4. Cost of the status quo
   - Connect pain to measurable risk, wasted time, lost revenue, compliance exposure, poor customer experience, or missed execution.

5. New standard or category point of view
   - Define what good now looks like.
   - Own the problem/category in plain language.

6. Solution introduction
   - Say what you sell only after the buyer understands the problem.
   - Connect the product directly to the new standard.

7. How it works
   - Show the core workflow or architecture.
   - Keep it simple enough for a first call.

8. Demo moment
   - Move into product early enough to create belief.
   - Use realistic data and a buyer-relevant scenario.

9. Proof
   - Use customer story, quote, metric, logo, benchmark, or before/after.
   - Be explicit about what the proof demonstrates.

10. Why us / why now
    - Clarify differentiation against the status quo and competitors.
    - Address the buyer's likely objection.

11. Commercial path
    - Include packaging, pricing shape, implementation, pilot path, or buying process when appropriate.

12. Recap and CTA
    - Summarize the pain, value, proof, and next step.
    - Ask for a specific action: technical validation, stakeholder meeting, pilot scoping, business case, or proposal.

Adjust this structure based on the sales motion. A short transactional deck may need 6-8 slides. An enterprise deck may need appendix support for security, architecture, implementation, pricing, and competition.

## Demo guidance

A strong sales narrative deck should not hide the product until the end.

Demo rules:

- Get to product early, usually after the problem and point-of-view slides.
- Use realistic data that matches the buyer's world.
- Show one coherent workflow, not a feature inventory.
- Pause after important moments and confirm the audience follows.
- Ask discovery questions while showing product, for example:
  - "How does your team handle this today?"
  - "Where does this usually break down?"
  - "Who needs to trust this output before it moves forward?"
- If the audience looks confused, stop and clarify before continuing.
- Recap what the demo proved before moving to proof, pricing, or next steps.

## Quality bar

The deck is good when:

- A rep could use it in a real first call without rewriting the whole story.
- The opening feels immediately relevant to the target audience.
- The story starts with pains, needs, and current solutions before the product.
- The deck educates the buyer before it pitches.
- The company clearly owns a problem or category.
- Each slide naturally follows the previous slide.
- Slide titles tell the story when read by themselves.
- No slide relies on more than three bullets to make its point.
- Product, proof, and pricing appear at the right level of depth for the sales motion.
- The demo moment is specific, realistic, and tied to discovery.
- Practitioners see their daily pain; leaders see business value and risk reduction.
- The CTA is concrete and appropriate for the stage.

## Anti-patterns

Avoid:

- Opening with company history, logos, or product architecture before the buyer cares.
- Saying what you sell before naming the audience's problem and current alternatives.
- Creating a generic deck that could fit any SaaS company.
- Treating different buyers as if they share the same pains, needs, and GTM motion.
- Overbuilding a first-call deck with every feature, proof point, and roadmap idea.
- Saving the product demo until the last five minutes.
- Using fake precision or unsupported ROI claims.
- Writing slide titles like "Problem," "Solution," or "Demo" instead of message-led headlines.
- Stuffing slides with more than three bullets.
- Building a beautiful narrative that sales cannot actually sell now.
- Ignoring confusion during the demo.
- Ending without a recap and clear next step.
